Sleep and Education

Oxford University 2015

Experiment

Earlier this year Dr. Paul Kelley of Oxford University came to the conclusion that, after studying thousands of circadian rhythms in individuals under the age of 55, the circadian rhythms of most adults are completely out of sync with normal working hours - something that poses a serious threat to mood and mental health.

In fact not only is this a detriment to health, but humans aren't even capable of doing their best work or focusing enough when forced to start on such early schedules. For optimal performance, Kelley suggests that teenagers should begin their day at 10 am, while university students and beyond should begin at 11 am.

Effects

Kelley's hypothesis - that moving school times could substantially raise grades - was put to the test when he had the opportunity to change the schedule of a local middle school. After changing the school start time from 8:30 am to 10 am, administrators found that the number of top grades rose by 19%.

As a continuation of Kelley's study, Oxford University has developed an experiment that will being in early 2016, where tens of thousands of children will begin school at 10 am to prove that later classes can improve exam results.
