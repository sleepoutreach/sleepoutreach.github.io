Appetite Control

With the prevalence of obesity in the U.S. increasing in the last two decades, extensive exploration shows that sleep deprivation is a major contributor. Studies show that our a body undergoing sleep deprivation exhibits increased levels of ghrelin, the "hunger" hormone, and decreased levels of leptin, the "I'm-full" hormone. This means we end up eating more than we need to when we are sleep deprived. We also end up craving high carbohydrate foods like sweets, starchy food, and salty food.

Glucose Tolerance

Sleep deprivation shows a glucose metabolism similar to that of someone with Type 2 Diabetes. When participants in a study were tested after sleep deprivation, they took 40% longer than normal to regulate blood sugar levels after high-carbohydrate meals. The body's response to insulin and insulin secretion decreases by 30% which is incredibly similar to Type 2 Diabetes. Sleep deprivation also changes the production of other hormones like lowering the secretion of thyroid stimulating hormone and increasing blood levels of cortisol.
